My Methodology

Thoroughout my projects, I ask myself a couple of questions.

What is this project supposed to do?

A lot of programmers I’m friends with love to comment on my coding style. I create best by throwing my previous knowledge at a digital wall, building small pieces of my project out of the things I like, and scrapping the others. While this style enables me to be more creative and solve problems considerably faster than my programming peers, It can also potentially cause me to get sidetracked and create beautiful programs that are unfortunately entirely different than what I had set out to make. In order to stay on track, I constantly ask myself “What is this project supposed to do?” Doing this forces me into making tiny adjustments to my projects, fine-tuning them until they perfectly fulfill their purposes’.

How can one benefit from this project?

While I can understand the appeal, I’ve never wanted to work at one of the main tech companies. I’ve always thought that computer science could be used for something more than fine-tuning privacy intruding algorithms and developing UX that corrodes the average human attention span. In an effort to keep in line with these values, I ask myself whenever I create a prototype: “Why did I create this?” If the answer to this question involves improving a community, the well-being of others, or my own skill, then I continue working on it. While I don't expect myself to change the world with each project, I want my work to have some degree of meaning. As the saying goes: “Look to solve problems, not create solutions.”
